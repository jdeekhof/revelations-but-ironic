whats my favorite bit of software that I've written

The first time that I felt like I really understood classes and instances was when I spent the better part of an entire night abstracting the process of putting different types of data into certain cells on a spreadsheet. It was also the first time that I really understood what good software architecture really means. The idea is that you decide and delegate behavior to their core classes. The main problem I see when people try to do this is that they are starting from a 5000 foot level and abstracting from the top down. The issue with this is that you will need to rewrite it 50 times in order to get to the true primitive that exists at the very bottom of the project.

The project I worked on was filling spreadsheets with various types of data. Making the spreadsheet consisted of adding each datum row by row. The first solution was really messy. We added datum row by row with a long list of cells inside of that `.add_row`. This means if we ever wanted to move a column to another place, we would effectively need to rewrite the entire file along with the headers. The next solution was a lot more nifty. We used procs to continue adding by row, but now a certain function would be called based on the column header. This allowed us the flexibility to move columsn really easily, but placing data in non-repeating fashion was now incredibly difficult and it was impossible to read. My solution, which was my favorite :), abstracted away the placement and allowed classes to own that detail.

The solution drew off of the CSV class we used. I made a sheet class, a row class, and a column class. The column belonged to the row and the row belonged to the sheet. Now, placing data was as easy as declaring variables. Here's an example of placing some data in Sheet1!A1. 'sheet_1 = Sheet.new; a = sheet1.add_column!(name: 'Column Header Name'); one = a.add_row!; a.one.insert('some_data')'. There were some drawbacks, but now we could place data or rows with minimal effort.

language and understanding

Have you ever heard or had someone talk about the life of a dog? I find the tinge of jealousy that comes along with the thought of not having a single responsibilities so fascinating. Don't get me wrong, this is something I've joked about as well. I presume many wonder if they really mean it though. Its a simple metaphor that almost anyone can understand. The dog's life is a visceral foil that we're using to convey something we all feel from time to time: overwhelmed.

It's just a little ironic. We are talking and fantasizing about a life without being able to talk or fantasize. Sure, dogs do have an understanding of language and barks, but could it ever joke about a caterpillar's life to another? A monkey with a typewriter and infinite time would produce all of Shakespeare's works, as well as endless amounts of absolute nonsense. Further, it would never understand the difference between the two. The unimaginable complexity of the fact that electrical signals in our brain causes our diaphragm to contract at frequencies to produces sound waves that represents words that form sentences that convey thoughts in other's ears is unbelievable. People argue about whether things like telekinesis and mind reading exist, not realizing that they are actively participating in it at that very moment.

I wonder sometimes about the limits of language and how they relate to the limit of modern day thought. Shakespeare invented several words, did the feelings/ideas exist prior? In other words, did the chicken or the egg come first? Did the chicken and the egg pre-exist that colloquialism? People for centuries have experienced the cold-start problem on every single level of society. So I ask again, did the chicken come first or was it the egg. It was neither: the environment came first.

why do we fly at 14,000 feet in airplanes?

I saw a reddit post the other day about a young, eager employee at a major airline, let's call them Bob. Bob had come up with an idea to potentially save the entire industry millions. Bob wanted to spin up the aircraft's tires prior to landing so that the tread would not be so worn after every landing. Instead of ten landings on a set of tires, they'd be able to do hundreds! He arrived one day and proudly told his manager his idea. His disappointment was swiftly delivered. Many have come before asking the same question and the answer was the same: its a lot of unneccesary weight to add which will cost tons of fuel over the long term. Its another system that will need maintenance to prevent catastrophic failure. Static tires will wear more but its cheaper and safer than trying to spin them up to 200 mph. Basically, Bob's idea was dead on arrival. I think most of us can think of a story similar to Bob's from a co-worker or maybe even your managee (or maybe it was you). This story and yours just go to show how difficult it is to actually challenge convention. It's evidence that individuality and uniqueness is actually exceedingly difficult. I wager that it's growing more difficult by the day. I say this because I see a growing population, and also because our days are more and more mechanized.

I don't really want to comment at this time on why I think the world is more mechanized than ever before. It seems like an obvious truth to be honest and overly cynical to fully explain.

The world is growing at the moment. We are currently at around 8 billion. The more competition, the more difficult something becomes. Take baseball for example, back when Babe Ruth played, the sport was largely a second job for most. Many played for a love of the game. The sport grew and grew. More people began watching and more began playing. Skip to today, and now you need to dedicate your life from the age of ~4 to ~25 to the sport to even have a chance to play in the big leagues. The same goes for being unique. I know, I know. Baseball is a closed game with only so many positions, how can you possibly liken that to the seemingly infinite possibilities of human capability. We have people going to space: that's entirely new and unique so you must be wrong. I concede, you win.

Just kidding, you should know better by now. If you examine the medium that the explorer is exploring and only within this context, you are correct; spacemen are entirely new. But we don't live in a vacuum (too good to pass up), and explorers have existed since the dawn of time. Sure, space has it's own very unique challenges versus something like say exploring the ocean blue, but is it really all that different or unique in terms of the human perspective? They are going to a place that is unknown. We haven't truly explored the bottom of the ocean yet, do you think that those individuals are so different than modern day astronauts? To go back to the baseball comparison, it's in the same ballpark. It's still the same game.

So back to Bob right? What's he to do? Be frustrated and give up? Press forward and keep working? I guess that depends on who Bob is and what he actually values. Bob, can you tell me why airplanes fly so high? Circumference is equal to pi * R^2! For every mile higher in the air, we are parabolically increasing the circumference to our destination. I bet its because of the air. It's so much less dense at 14,000 feet and even though we are going farther, the fuel to power the turbines goes a lot further.
